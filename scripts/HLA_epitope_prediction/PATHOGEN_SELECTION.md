# Pathogen Selection for European Population HLA Analysis

## Overview

This document explains the selection of 20 pathogens for HLA epitope analysis, focusing on infectious diseases that exerted significant selective pressure on European populations over the last 10,000 years.

---

## Selection Criteria

1. **Historical prevalence** in Europe over 10,000 years
2. **Mortality rate** and disease severity
3. **Endemic vs. epidemic** patterns
4. **Age of pathogen-human interaction** (ancient vs. recent)
5. **Documented impact** on European demographics
6. **Zoonotic origin** related to agricultural revolution

---

## Pathogens by Category

### **VIRAL PATHOGENS (4 pathogens)**

#### 1. **Smallpox** (Variola virus)
- **Reference strain**: Bangladesh-1975
- **Historical impact**:
  - 300-500 million deaths in 20th century alone
  - 30% mortality rate
  - Major killer in Europe from Roman times through 18th century
  - Drove HLA selection (e.g., HLA-B*07:02 resistance documented)
- **Timeline**: Ancient disease, possibly 10,000+ years
- **Selective pressure**: **EXTREME** - one of the strongest known

#### 2. **HIV** (Human immunodeficiency virus)
- **Reference strain**: HXB2 (THE HIV reference for sequence numbering)
- **Historical impact**:
  - Recent pathogen (1980s+) but included for comparison
  - Strong HLA associations (HLA-B*27, B*57 protective)
  - Demonstrates ongoing HLA selection
- **Timeline**: 1980s - present
- **Selective pressure**: **HIGH** (recent but intense)

#### 3. **Measles**
- **Reference strain**: Edmonston (vaccine strain)
- **Historical impact**:
  - Ancient disease, endemic in Europe
  - Major childhood killer (10-30% mortality in outbreaks)
  - Affected population structure through childhood mortality
- **Timeline**: ~2500 BCE onwards (after cities formed)
- **Selective pressure**: **HIGH**

#### 4. **Hepatitis B**
- **Reference strain**: subtype adw2 (common in Europe)
- **Historical impact**:
  - Ancient chronic infection
  - Cirrhosis, liver cancer
  - Vertical transmission shapes population structure
  - Strong HLA associations with clearance vs. chronic infection
- **Timeline**: Ancient (>10,000 years)
- **Selective pressure**: **MODERATE to HIGH**

---

### **BACTERIAL PATHOGENS - MAJOR HISTORICAL (5 pathogens)**

#### 5. **Plague** (Yersinia pestis)
- **Reference strain**: CO92
- **Historical impact**:
  - Black Death (1347-1353): Killed 30-60% of European population
  - Justinian Plague (541-549 CE): 25-50 million deaths
  - Multiple subsequent outbreaks through 18th century
  - **Single greatest demographic catastrophe in European history**
- **Timeline**: Emerged ~5000 years ago, hit Europe ~541 CE
- **Selective pressure**: **EXTREME** - strongest recent selection event

#### 6. **Tuberculosis** (Mycobacterium tuberculosis)
- **Reference strain**: H37Rv (THE TB reference strain)
- **Historical impact**:
  - "White plague" - leading cause of death in 19th century Europe
  - 20-25% of all deaths in some European cities
  - Ancient disease, strong HLA associations
  - Chronic infection allows strong selection
- **Timeline**: Ancient (>10,000 years with humans)
- **Selective pressure**: **EXTREME** - prolonged, high mortality

#### 7. **Leprosy** (Mycobacterium leprae)
- **Reference strain**: TN
- **Historical impact**:
  - Major medieval disease in Europe (6th-13th centuries)
  - Strong stigma led to social isolation
  - HLA strongly influences susceptibility (HLA-DR2, DQ1)
  - Declined in Europe but was prevalent for centuries
- **Timeline**: Ancient in Europe (Roman times - medieval)
- **Selective pressure**: **MODERATE** - chronic, lower mortality but strong selection on susceptibility

#### 8. **Typhoid Fever** (Salmonella typhi)
- **Reference strain**: CT18
- **Historical impact**:
  - Major waterborne killer throughout history
  - "Typhoid Mary" - carrier states
  - Camp fever in armies (often confused with typhus)
  - 10-30% mortality untreated
- **Timeline**: Ancient, increased with urbanization
- **Selective pressure**: **HIGH**

#### 9. **Syphilis** (Treponema pallidum)
- **Reference strain**: Nichols (classical reference since 1912)
- **Historical impact**:
  - Post-1493 epidemic in Europe (Columbian Exchange hypothesis)
  - Virulent epidemic form in Renaissance
  - Major cause of insanity, death in early modern period
  - "Great pox" vs smallpox
- **Timeline**: 1493 CE onwards in Europe
- **Selective pressure**: **HIGH** (intense but shorter timeframe)

---

### **BACTERIAL PATHOGENS - RESPIRATORY (2 pathogens)**

#### 10. **Pneumonia** (Streptococcus pneumoniae)
- **Reference strain**: TIGR4
- **Historical impact**:
  - Leading cause of death in elderly and children historically
  - "Captain of the Men of Death" (William Osler)
  - Major contributor to infant/childhood mortality
  - 30-40% of pneumonias historically
- **Timeline**: Ancient
- **Selective pressure**: **HIGH** - continuous, high mortality

#### 11. **Diphtheria** (Corynebacterium diphtheriae)
- **Reference strain**: NCTC 13129
- **Historical impact**:
  - Major childhood killer in 18th-19th century Europe
  - 10% mortality rate (50% in young children)
  - Epidemic disease in urban areas
  - "Strangling angel of children"
- **Timeline**: Ancient, peaked 18th-19th centuries
- **Selective pressure**: **MODERATE to HIGH**

---

### **BACTERIAL PATHOGENS - STREPTOCOCCAL (1 pathogen)**

#### 12. **Scarlet Fever / Puerperal Fever** (Streptococcus pyogenes)
- **Reference strain**: M1 GAS (most common serotype globally)
- **Historical impact**:
  - Puerperal fever: 10-30% maternal mortality in 19th century hospitals
  - Scarlet fever: major childhood killer
  - Necrotizing fasciitis: rapid tissue destruction
  - One of the leading causes of maternal death historically
- **Timeline**: Ancient, but epidemic scarlet fever emerged ~17th century
- **Selective pressure**: **HIGH** - especially on women of reproductive age

---

### **BACTERIAL PATHOGENS - VECTOR-BORNE (2 pathogens)**

#### 13. **Epidemic Typhus** (Rickettsia prowazekii)
- **Reference strain**: Madrid E
- **Historical impact**:
  - "War fever" - major killer in armies, sieges, prisons
  - Defeated Napoleon's Grande ArmÃ©e in Russia (1812)
  - 3 million deaths in Russia/Eastern Europe (1918-1922)
  - Famine and war-associated
- **Timeline**: Ancient, but major European epidemics 16th-20th centuries
- **Selective pressure**: **HIGH** during epidemic periods

#### 14. **Relapsing Fever** (Borrelia recurrentis)
- **Reference strain**: A1
- **Historical impact**:
  - Louse-borne epidemic disease
  - Famine and war-associated
  - Episodic relapses from antigenic variation
  - Major epidemics in Ireland (famine), Eastern Europe
- **Timeline**: Ancient, epidemic in historical Europe
- **Selective pressure**: **MODERATE** - episodic

---

### **BACTERIAL PATHOGENS - ZOONOTIC (3 pathogens)**

These pathogens emerged with the agricultural revolution and animal domestication (~8,000-10,000 years ago in Europe).

#### 15. **Anthrax** (Bacillus anthracis)
- **Reference strain**: Ames
- **Historical impact**:
  - Occupational disease of farmers, herders, wool workers
  - "5th plague of Egypt" (possibly)
  - Cutaneous, inhalational, and GI forms
  - Robert Koch's proof of germ theory used anthrax
- **Timeline**: Ancient agricultural disease (~10,000 years)
- **Selective pressure**: **MODERATE** - occupational, lower prevalence

#### 16. **Brucellosis** (Brucella melitensis)
- **Reference strain**: 16M (most pathogenic to humans)
- **Historical impact**:
  - "Undulant fever" from livestock (cattle, goats, sheep)
  - Chronic debilitating disease
  - Associated with dairy farming and herding
  - Ancient occupational zoonosis
- **Timeline**: Ancient agricultural disease (~10,000 years)
- **Selective pressure**: **MODERATE** - chronic, agricultural populations

#### 17. **Listeriosis** (Listeria monocytogenes)
- **Reference strain**: EGD-e
- **Historical impact**:
  - Foodborne illness, particularly from dairy and meat
  - Major cause of spontaneous abortion
  - Neonatal meningitis
  - High mortality in vulnerable populations (30%)
- **Timeline**: Ancient agricultural disease
- **Selective pressure**: **MODERATE** - especially affects pregnancy outcomes

---

### **BACTERIAL PATHOGENS - WATERBORNE (2 pathogens)**

#### 18. **Cholera** (Vibrio cholerae)
- **Reference strain**: O1 biovar El Tor str. N16961
- **Historical impact**:
  - Seven cholera pandemics since 1817
  - Major 19th century killer in Europe
  - Led to sanitation reforms
  - 50% mortality untreated
- **Timeline**: Ancient in Asia, hit Europe 19th-20th centuries
- **Selective pressure**: **HIGH** during epidemics (but recent in Europe)

#### 19. **Leptospirosis** (Leptospira interrogans)
- **Reference strain**: Copenhageni str. Fiocruz L1-130
- **Historical impact**:
  - "Weil's disease"
  - Occupational disease (farmers, sewer workers, miners)
  - Waterborne from rodent urine
  - Jaundice, kidney failure
- **Timeline**: Ancient, agricultural/urban disease
- **Selective pressure**: **MODERATE** - occupational/environmental

---

### **PARASITIC PATHOGENS (1 pathogen)**

#### 20. **Malaria vivax** (Plasmodium vivax)
- **Reference strain**: Sal-1
- **Historical impact**:
  - Ancient in Mediterranean and southern Europe
  - Major selective pressure (Duffy antigen, G6PD deficiency)
  - Endemic in southern Europe through 20th century
  - "Roman fever" contributed to fall of Rome
  - **Strongest known HLA selection** (HLA-B*53, others in Africa)
- **Timeline**: Ancient (>10,000 years)
- **Selective pressure**: **EXTREME** - one of strongest in human history

---

## Additional Pathogens Considered But Not Included

### Not Included (Insufficient European Impact or Recent):
- **Poliovirus**: Ancient but lower mortality, primarily paralysis
- **Influenza**: High mortality but antigenically variable (hard to define reference)
- **Varicella-zoster (Chickenpox)**: High prevalence but low mortality
- **Rubella**: Lower mortality, primarily birth defects
- **EBV, CMV, HSV**: Near-universal chronic infections but lower mortality
- **Neisseria meningitidis**: Epidemic but lower overall impact
- **Bordetella pertussis (Whooping cough)**: Childhood disease but included in vaccines, less ancient selection

### Borderline (Could Add in Future):
- **Staphylococcus aureus**: Chronic infections, modern concern (MRSA)
- **Plasmodium falciparum**: More deadly than P. vivax but less common in Europe historically
- **Toxoplasma gondii**: Ancient but unclear selective pressure

## Timeline of Pathogen Introductions to Europe

### **Paleolithic (>10,000 BCE)**
- Tuberculosis (with early humans)
- Malaria (ancient)
- Hepatitis B (ancient)

### **Neolithic / Agricultural Revolution (10,000 - 3,000 BCE)**
- Anthrax (livestock)
- Brucellosis (livestock)
- Listeria (livestock/agriculture)
- Salmonella typhi (urbanization)
- Leptospira (agriculture)

### **Bronze/Iron Age (3,000 BCE - 500 CE)**
- Leprosy (trade routes)
- Measles (urbanization, ~2500 BCE)
- Plague (emerged ~3000 BCE, hit Europe later)

### **Classical/Medieval (500 CE - 1500 CE)**
- Justinian Plague (541 CE)
- Black Death plague (1347 CE)
- Leprosy epidemic (medieval peak)
- Relapsing fever (epidemics)
- Streptococcal diseases (puerperal fever documented)

### **Early Modern (1500 - 1900 CE)**
- Syphilis (1493 CE - Columbian Exchange)
- Epidemic typhus (war/famine)
- Cholera pandemics (19th century)
- Diphtheria epidemics
- Scarlet fever epidemics
- Smallpox (continued)

### **Modern (1900 - present)**
- HIV (1980s)

## Implications for Analysis

### **Core Selection Events** (Strongest Expected Signal):
1. Plague (Black Death) - 14th century bottleneck
2. Tuberculosis - continuous high mortality
3. Smallpox - continuous high mortality
4. Malaria - southern Europe, strong selection
5. Measles - childhood mortality

### **Secondary Selection** (Moderate Signal):
6-12. Typhoid, Strep infections, Pneumonia, Syphilis, Typhus, Leprosy, Hepatitis B

### **Tertiary/Recent** (Weaker or More Recent Signal):
13-20. Remaining pathogens

### **Population-Specific Signatures**:
- **Northern Europe**: TB, plague, typhus, smallpox
- **Southern Europe**: Malaria, plague, TB, leprosy
- **Agricultural populations**: Zoonotic diseases
- **Urban populations**: Waterborne and respiratory diseases

## Reference Strain Rationale

All strains selected are:
1. **Well-annotated** reference genomes
2. **Historically relevant** to European populations
3. **Standard references** in the literature
4. **Complete genomes** available

For pathogens with high strain variation (e.g., Strep pneumoniae, HIV), we selected the most common or best-studied reference strain.

---

## Analysis Strategy

### **Phase 1**: All 20 pathogens (current scope)
### **Phase 2**: Compare HLA binding profiles across:
- Time periods (ancient vs. recent)
- Geographic distribution (north vs. south)
- Transmission mode (respiratory, vector, zoonotic)
- Disease severity (mortality rates)

### **Phase 3**: Correlate with:
- European HLA allele frequencies
- Geographic clines in HLA distribution
- Known HLA disease associations
- Ancient DNA data from European skeletons

---

## Expected Outcomes

1. **Identify pathogen-specific HLA signatures**
2. **Compare shared vs. unique epitopes** across pathogens
3. **Test hypotheses** about HLA frequency distributions in Europe
4. **Prioritize alleles** showing strongest pathogen associations
5. **Generate predictions** for future pathogen HLA studies
